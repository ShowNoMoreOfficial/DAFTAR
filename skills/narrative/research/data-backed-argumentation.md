## Instructions

Build arguments that are logically sound and supported by data at every step.

### Argument Structure

**Claim → Evidence → Implication → Connection**

1. **Claim**: State the position clearly ("India's semiconductor dependency is a national security risk")
2. **Evidence**: Back it with specific data from the dossier ("India imports 100% of its chips, worth $38 billion in 2025")
3. **Implication**: Explain why this matters ("A single supply chain disruption could shut down India's IT sector for weeks")
4. **Connection**: Link to the broader narrative ("This is why the India Semiconductor Mission's ₹76,000 crore investment matters")

### Data Presentation Rules
- **Be specific**: "₹4.2 lakh crore" is better than "billions of rupees"
- **Use comparisons**: "That's equivalent to India's entire defense budget" — makes numbers meaningful
- **Show trends**: "Up 34% from last year" — direction matters more than absolute numbers
- **Use per-capita or per-unit**: "That's ₹3,000 per Indian household" — personalizes macro data

### Visualization Opportunities
Flag data points that would work as visual elements:
- Time series → line chart or animated counter
- Comparisons → bar chart or split-screen
- Geographic data → heat map or map overlay
- Proportions → pie chart or stacked bar
- Before/after → side-by-side with change indicator

### Logical Fallacy Check
Before finalizing, verify the argument doesn't commit:
- Correlation ≠ causation
- Cherry-picked time periods
- Survivorship bias
- False dichotomy ("either we do X or disaster")
- Appeal to authority without data
